## Key Technical Insights

### Peptide Design Principles

- **Small molecules are generally easier for EPA approval** than biologics — useful framing for regulatory conversations
- **Spinosad** (spinosicide) cited as one of the most commercially successful biopesticides — produced by bacteria via fermentation, non-toxic profile
- Two mechanistic categories:
  - **Defensive** — block/protect receptors
  - **Offensive** — directly kill the pest
- **Finding effective peptide biopesticides is not the hard problem. Cost of production is.**

### Structural Constraints

- Many effective peptides have multiple cysteine residues that create disulfide bonds — these hold 3D structure but dramatically increase production cost and complexity
- **Goal: minimize cysteine count, keep structure stable** — fewer disulfide bonds = cheaper, easier to produce
- There are many effective defensive peptides **without** disulfide bonds — prioritize these
- For insecticides: bonds need to be **stable in the gut environment** — critical constraint for oral delivery

### Insect Target Categories

- **Lepidoptera, Coleoptera, Hemiptera** — the three major insect pest categories to design for

### Fungicide

- Making an antifungal peptide that works in the cell is not technically hard — many approaches exist
- **The failure mode is cost of production**, not efficacy discovery

## Production & Manufacturing

- **Yeast is far safer than E. coli as a production host** — ask Bowman why (didn't fully catch the reasoning — follow up)
- **E. coli has a bad reputation** in the industry; filamentous fungi are not necessarily slower as hosts
- **Geltor** (Bay Area) — produces proteins but charges a lot; not the right partner for early-stage
- **Target production cost: <$50,000/kg** — rough industry benchmark for viability
- **Rough market rate: ~$1M/kg** for custom proteins at research scale — confirms early synthesis will be expensive
- For trial-phase production: small fermentation companies exist that can produce at reasonable cost; **$2–3M is sufficient to get a small fermentation company to produce for you**
- **Columns for purification are problematic at scale** — avoid purification-heavy approaches
- Broad-acre proteins typically produced via: secretory expression + freeze-drying + compression, or solvent-based methods

## The "Winner" Peptide Profile

His framing of what a commercially viable peptide looks like:

> Picomolar-range potency, highly soluble, secretable — if you find that, you have something.

- **Potency:** picomolar range binding/activity
- **Solubility:** high — insoluble peptides cannot be formulated or assayed
- **Secretability:** the production host needs to secrete it (avoids expensive extraction/purification)
