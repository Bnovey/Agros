## 1. US Regulatory Pathways

### The Three Tracks (fastest → slowest)

#### Track 1: EPA 25(b) Minimum Risk Exemption — Fastest, no federal registration
- Products using only EPA-approved active and inert ingredients are exempt from FIFRA registration
- No federal registration fee, no EPA review required — sell state-by-state immediately
- Key constraint: active ingredient must be on the approved 25(b) list (natural, low-toxicity substances)
- **For Agros:** If our peptides can be characterized as naturally occurring or derived from natural sources, 25(b) is the fastest path to market. Check the current active ingredient list carefully.
- OMRI listing (for organic market access) is separate and can run in parallel

#### Track 2: EPA Biopesticide Registration (Biochemical) — Best fit for novel peptides
- Handled by EPA's Biopesticides and Pollution Prevention Division (BPPD), established 1994 specifically to expedite biopesticide registration
- Target review time: **~2 years** (vs. 5–7 years for conventional pesticide registration)
- Data requirements are substantially lower than conventional pesticides — no full mammalian toxicology battery required
- PRIA fees for biopesticide new active ingredient registration are far lower than conventional ($X vs. $XXX thousand — check current PRIA fee schedule)
- **Recent precedent directly relevant to us:** EPA just registered **Ea peptide 91398** (PHC-91398) — a naturally-derived peptide active ingredient that induces plant defenses for broad agricultural and residential use. This proves the regulatory pathway works for peptides.
- Expedited review incentive: biopesticides get priority in the queue — this is a genuine regulatory moat vs. synthetic competitors

#### Track 3: Conventional Pesticide Registration — Avoid unless forced
- 5–7 years, multi-million dollar data package
- Only relevant if peptide has off-target toxicity concerns that preclude biochemical biopesticide classification

### IP and Data Exclusivity
- EPA provides a **10-year data exclusivity period** for biopesticide registrations — competitors can't use your safety data
- **Patent filing must occur before bioRxiv preprint** — once prior art is established, you lose novelty claims
- Patent what you can: specific peptide sequences, method of synthesis, formulation, field of use, and critically the AI-guided discovery method itself
- Trade secret the rest: model architecture, training data, IG analysis workflow — these don't need to be patented and can be protected indefinitely
- Key challenge: peptides that closely resemble naturally occurring sequences may fail novelty/non-obviousness — your AI-designed de novo sequences should be sufficiently novel, but get a patent attorney's opinion early

## 2. International Scaling Strategy

### Priority Market Order

#### 1. Canada — Immediate parallel to US (fastest expansion)
- Pest Management Regulatory Agency (PMRA) has a science-based, proportionate-risk framework
- **Mutual recognition:** If EPA has registered a biopesticide, PMRA can use that data package for provisional or full registration
- High overlap in pest pressures and specialty crops with US market
- Timeline: 12–18 months after US registration with same data package

#### 2. Brazil — Largest near-term growth opportunity
- World's largest biopesticide growth market: 42% increase in consumption 2017–2020 (vs. 16% global)
- Government "ABC Plus" credit lines: low-interest loans to farmers adopting biological inputs — growers are actively incentivized to switch
- Regulatory fast-track approvals specifically for biopesticides have been enacted
- Brazil is among the world's largest specialty and commodity crop exporters — massive scale
- **Risk:** regulatory process has its own idiosyncrasies; partnering with a local distributor or agchem player is likely required
- Timeline: 18–30 months from first US registration with a Brazilian partner

#### 3. EU — Biggest regulatory prize, now with a fast-track
- European Commission published December 16, 2025: proposed amendments to **Regulation 1107/2009** introducing:
  - Legal definition of biocontrol active substances (first time)
  - Accelerated/priority approval procedure for biocontrol substances
  - Provisional product authorizations before full EU approval
  - Tacit agreement rule: if member states don't decide within 120 days, authorization is deemed granted
  - EFSA (European Food Safety Authority) priority processing for biocontrol applicants
- **Why this matters:** EU approval previously took 7–9 years and cost £3–5M. Reform is targeting 2–3 years. This unlocks the EU market dramatically.
- EU Farm to Fork 50% synthetic pesticide reduction target remains policy goal — regulatory push is real even if the SUR legislation was abandoned
- European Parliament report (A10-0234/2025) explicitly calls for faster registration and uptake of biological control agents
- Timeline to enter EU: 3–4 years post-reform implementation (2027–2028 earliest realistic), but **begin EFSA pre-submission meetings now** — the queue will get long

#### 4. Australia / New Zealand
- Science-based regulatory framework (APVMA in Australia)
- If product is already registered by US EPA, EU, UK, Japan, or Australia, **toxicological risk assessment reports can be submitted in support of provisional registration** in many jurisdictions
- This creates a domino effect: US registration unlocks simplified entry into AU/NZ
- Specialty wine grape and horticultural market — high-value crops, Botrytis is a major problem in AU/NZ wine regions

#### 5. India and China — Long-term, partner-required
- **India:** Central Insecticides Board (CIB) regulates. Guidelines tailored to chemical pesticides, causing delays for biopesticides. Government aiming to convert 1M hectares to natural farming by 2026 — tailwind. Best entered via a local partner.
- **China:** Ministry of Agriculture and Rural Affairs + SAMR. Dedicated biopesticide registration process. Government subsidies for biopesticide producers. Large market but IP risk is high — protect IP aggressively before entering.

## 3. Political Risks and Tailwinds

### Risks

#### Risk 1: Trump EPA deregulation reduces urgency for biopesticides
- The administration is rolling back safety reviews for hundreds of synthetic pesticides, delaying reviews until 2031
- Glyphosate executive order pushes for expanded production
- Re-approval of dicamba (previously struck down by courts)
- **Implication for Agros:** If synthetic pesticides face less regulatory pressure in the US, the *regulatory* urgency for growers to switch is reduced. This could slow US market adoption.
- **Mitigation:** Resistance pressure is biology, not regulation — even if the EPA removes some barriers, Botrytis resistance to conventional fungicides doesn't go away. Growers are losing tools regardless of political environment.

#### Risk 2: Federal science funding cuts
- The current administration has cut or frozen many federal R&D programs
- USDA NIFA SBIR funding depends on appropriations — if Congress reduces NIFA budget, Phase I/II rounds could shrink or be delayed
- NSF SBIR has also faced political pressure
- **Mitigation:** Apply for SBIR funding NOW, during YC batch, before the funding landscape worsens. Don't rely on federal grants as primary runway — treat them as supplemental non-dilutive capital.

#### Risk 3: Trade tensions increasing synthesis costs
- Tariffs on Chinese imports could increase the cost of peptide synthesis reagents (amino acids, resins, solvents) if suppliers are China-based
- Custom peptide CROs in China (where significant synthesis capacity is located) could become more expensive
- **Mitigation:** Diversify synthesis partners — US-based (AAPPTEC), European (ProteoGenix), and Indian CROs are alternatives. For early-stage R&D volumes, domestic pricing is manageable.

#### Risk 4: EU regulatory uncertainty
- The SUR (Sustainable Use Regulation) — which would have made the 50% pesticide reduction target legally binding — was abandoned in 2024 under political pressure from farmers
- New 1107/2009 reform is positive but still being negotiated — timeline and final scope uncertain
- EU political landscape has shifted rightward in 2024–2025, creating some headwinds for aggressive environmental regulation
- **Mitigation:** The EU fast-track for biopesticides is driven by ag competitiveness, not just environmental politics — it has cross-partisan support because EU farmers want access to these tools. Monitor but don't count on a specific date.

#### Risk 5: MAHA inconsistency
- The MAHA (Make America Healthy Again) movement under RFK Kennedy has mixed signals: calls for reducing toxic pesticides, but the MAHA Commission has stopped short of recommending bans, calling only for more research
- The Trump administration is simultaneously deregulating synthetic pesticides while MAHA calls for their reduction — these are in direct conflict
- **Implication:** MAHA creates a consumer tailwind (demand for clean food) but may not translate into regulatory change for several years
- RFK Kennedy met with 140+ farm interests by September 2025 — signaling that the MAHA agenda will be moderated by agricultural economics

### Tailwinds

#### Tailwind 1: Biological resistance — regulation-independent
- Botrytis resistance is a biological reality that no political environment can reverse. 94% of isolates resistant to thiophanate-methyl. 63.5% resistant to ≥4 FRAC groups. Growers will adopt alternatives when their current tools stop working — regardless of who is in the White House.

#### Tailwind 2: EU fast-track reform (December 2025) — historic
- The December 2025 Regulation 1107/2009 amendment proposal is the most significant EU biopesticide regulatory change in decades. Provisional authorizations + 120-day tacit agreement rules change the EU market from a 7-9 year slog to a realistic 2-3 year path.

#### Tailwind 3: Consumer demand for clean food
- Bipartisan consumer awareness of pesticide residues (MAHA, organic food growth, retailer sustainability commitments) is driving specialty crop growers toward biopesticides regardless of regulation
- Premium grocery (Whole Foods, etc.) and EU export markets require reduced synthetic residues — growers follow market access requirements

#### Tailwind 4: Brazil growth
- Brazil's biopesticide market is growing 3× faster than the global average and is structurally supported by government credit programs — a large, policy-backed tailwind that is independent of US politics

## 4. Federal Funding — Apply During YC Batch

### Priority 1: USDA NIFA SBIR/STTR — Best fit for Agros

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Phase I award | Up to $175,000, 8 months (SBIR) or 12 months (STTR) |
| Phase II award | Up to $600,000, 24 months |
| FY2025 Phase II pool | ~$19.5M available |
| Best topic areas | Biotechnology (Topic 8.1), Crop protection (Topic 8.4), AI in agriculture |
| Why Agros fits | Our closed-loop AI-to-wet-lab pipeline is exactly the kind of high-risk, high-reward ag research NIFA SBIR was designed for |
| Key URL | nifa.usda.gov/grants/programs/sbir-sttr |

**Strategy:** Apply for Phase I to establish the SBIR relationship. A Phase I win dramatically improves Phase II odds. Phase II $600K is meaningful non-dilutive capital.

**Risk:** Current administration has been inconsistent about science funding. Apply now.

### Priority 2: NSF SBIR/STTR — Strong AI + biotech angle

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Phase I award | Up to $305,000, 6–18 months |
| Phase II award | Up to $2,000,000 |
| Best topic areas | Biological Technologies (AI + protein design angle), Agricultural Technologies |
| Why Agros fits | BoltzGen + Boltz-2 + IG analysis is fundamentally an AI/ML system — NSF funds the AI innovation story, not just the ag application |
| Key URL | seedfund.nsf.gov |

**Strategy:** Frame the NSF application around the ML/interpretability innovation (AI platform for peptide discovery) — not just the agricultural application. NSF funds the science; the ag application is the use case.

### Priority 3: EPA SBIR — Environmental angle

- EPA runs its own SBIR program for environmental technologies
- Biopesticides as replacements for harmful synthetic pesticides fits the environmental protection mandate
- Less well-known than USDA/NSF SBIR — less competition
- Check current solicitations: epa.gov/sbir

### Priority 4: DOE ARPA-E (lower fit, worth watching)
- ARPA-E focuses primarily on energy, but has funded some biotech with energy relevance
- AgARDA (USDA's ARPA-E equivalent) was authorized at $50M/year but only received $2M in reality — underfunded and unreliable
- **Verdict:** Don't spend significant time on ARPA-E for now

### Priority 5: California-specific programs
- California Department of Food and Agriculture (CDFA): Integrated Pest Management grants
- UC Division of Agriculture and Natural Resources: research partnership funding
- California has a regulatory environment friendlier to biopesticides than the federal baseline

### Non-Dilutive Funding Calendar (approximate)
- **USDA NIFA SBIR Phase I:** Applications typically open Jan–Mar annually. Watch for FY2026 RFA.
- **NSF SBIR Phase I:** Rolling applications with three annual cycles. Apply any cycle.
- **EPA SBIR:** Annual solicitation, typically released Q1.

## 5. YC Interview Q&A on Policy and Funding

**Q: How does the political environment affect your business?**
A: Short answer — less than you'd think. Our tailwind is biological, not regulatory. Botrytis resistance to conventional fungicides is spreading regardless of who's in office. Growers lose tools; they need alternatives. The EU fast-track reform of December 2025 is a major positive for our EU expansion. And the MAHA movement is pushing consumer demand for clean food in our direction. We're not dependent on any one regulatory environment.

**Q: What's your regulatory strategy?**
A: US first via EPA biochemical biopesticide pathway — target 2 years using the same process that just registered Ea peptide 91398 (a direct peptide precedent). In parallel, Canada via mutual recognition of EPA data. Then Brazil, where the market is growing fastest. Then EU once the 2025 reform fast-track is in place.

**Q: Are you applying for government grants?**
A: Yes — USDA NIFA SBIR Phase I ($175K, non-dilutive) and NSF SBIR Phase I ($305K, non-dilutive). Combined, that's $480K in non-dilutive capital on top of YC funding. We frame USDA as the ag application and NSF as the AI platform innovation — same work, two agency angles.

**Q: What's your IP strategy?**
A: Three layers. Patent the novel peptide sequences before our bioRxiv preprint goes live. Trade secret the AI model, training data, and IG analysis workflow — these don't need to be published to be protected. And leverage EPA's 10-year data exclusivity period: once we register, competitors can't use our safety data for a decade.

**Q: What if the EU regulatory environment reverses?**
A: The EU fast-track is driven by ag competitiveness (farmers need tools, EU is at a disadvantage vs. Americas and Asia), not just environmental politics — it has cross-partisan support. Even if the 50% pesticide reduction target softens, growers still want effective biopesticides because their synthetic tools are failing.
