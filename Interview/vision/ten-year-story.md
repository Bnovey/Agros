# Vision — The 10-Year Story

YC bets on big outcomes. "We'll build a nice biopesticide business" is not the answer. You need a clear, committed vision for what the world looks like if Agros wins — and it needs to be bigger than Botrytis.

---

## The Thesis (say this out loud until it's automatic)

> "Synthetic pesticides are going to fail across most of agriculture within 20 years — resistance is winning, regulation is tightening, and the discovery pipeline for new synthetic actives has been essentially empty for decades. Biopesticides will replace them, but the discovery process for biologicals is still artisanal. Agros is building the platform that makes biological discovery systematic — the same way combinatorial chemistry systematized small-molecule drug discovery in the 90s. In 10 years, every major crop disease and pest has a biological active designed by our platform, and the wet lab is closed."

---

## What the World Looks Like If Agros Wins

**In 5 years:**
- Agros has registered 2–3 novel peptide actives against major crop diseases (Botrytis, nematodes, insect pests)
- At least one licensing deal generating royalty revenue with a major agchem biologicals division
- The platform has been run against 5+ targets — proprietary experimental dataset is compounding
- BioRxiv preprint has become a peer-reviewed paper, establishing scientific credibility
- Series B funded, 10–15 person team (ML + wet lab)

**In 10 years:**
- Agros is the foundational discovery platform for biopesticide actives globally — the "pick and shovel" for the biologicals revolution in agriculture
- Portfolio of 10+ registered or in-development peptide actives across crop diseases, insects, and nematodes
- The closed-loop dataset (peptide sequence → wet lab activity → model improvement) is the deepest proprietary database of agricultural peptide activity in the world — moat no competitor can close without running the same experiments
- Synthetic pesticide use in specialty crops has dropped 30–40% — Agros actives are a significant part of the replacement stack
- Either: acquired by BASF/Bayer/Syngenta biologicals division at a significant premium, or a standalone public company with a biologicals product portfolio

## The Endgame Options (pick one to commit to, but know the others)

### Option A: Platform acquisition (most likely outcome in 10 years)
BASF, Bayer, Syngenta, Corteva, or a major agchem distributor acquires Agros for the platform and the proprietary dataset — not just the individual actives. Comparables: BASF's acquisition of Becker Underwood (~$1B), Bayer's acquisition of AgraQuest, Marrone Bio acquired by Bioceres. Strategic acquirers pay for the discovery engine, not just what it's produced so far.

### Option B: Standalone biologicals company
Agros builds a full product portfolio — direct-to-grower biopesticide products across specialty crops — and becomes the Marrone Bio / BioWorks of the AI-native generation, except with a platform moat instead of individual product IP. This is the longer, harder path but potentially the largest outcome.

### Option C: Biologicals discovery platform (SaaS / licensing hybrid)
Agros licenses access to the discovery platform itself — agchem companies pay to run their own targets through the pipeline. Combines licensing income (per active) with platform income (per discovery campaign). This is the "picks and shovels" model.

**Recommended answer to "what's the endgame?":**
> "We think the most likely 10-year outcome is acquisition — the major agchem biologicals divisions are buying discovery capability, not building it. The precedent is BASF buying Becker Underwood, Bayer buying AgraQuest. We're building the asset they'll want when the biologicals transition accelerates and they realize they can't build an interpretable ML-to-wet-lab pipeline internally in time. But if the licensing model scales faster than expected, we could become a standalone biologicals company. We're building for the big outcome either way."

## Why This Is the Right Moment

The combinatorial chemistry analogy is important: in the 1990s, combinatorial synthesis + HTS transformed small-molecule drug discovery from artisanal to systematic. The pharmaceutical industry was never the same. Agros is doing the same thing for biopesticide peptides — taking what is currently slow, expensive, and dependent on expert intuition, and making it fast, cheap, and systematic.

The difference from the 1990s analogy: **we understand the results, not just produce them.** Combinatorial HTS generated hits without mechanism; IG analysis gives us hits plus mechanism, which makes iteration faster and IP stronger.

## The "Why Will You Win?" Answer

> "We will win because the interpretability flywheel compounds in ways that pure screening doesn't. Every experiment we run makes the model smarter in a specific, directional way — not just 'remove this candidate from the list.' In 3 years, we'll have the deepest experimental dataset on agricultural peptide activity in the world, trained specifically on our targets. That dataset is the moat. Bindwell can scan all known compounds in 6 hours; they can't replicate 3 years of our wet lab data in any amount of time."
