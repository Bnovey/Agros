# Source summary: Baxter 2010 — Pxα6 spinosad resistance

## Bibliographic details

- Author: Baxter SW, Bansal R, Bhatt A, Bhatt DP, et al.
- Date: 2010
- Journal: *PLOS Genetics* 6(1): e1000802
- DOI: https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pgen.1000802
- Original file: `raw/papers/Baxter_2010_PxnAChRa6_spinosad_resistance.md`
- Type: Primary research article

## Summary

Spinosad resistance in multiple field populations of *Plutella xylostella* across different continents is caused by aberrant splicing of the nAChR α6 subunit gene (Pxα6). Resistant populations share a common molecular mechanism — exon skipping in the Pxα6 transcript produces truncated, non-functional receptor subunits that cannot assemble into spinosad-sensitive pentameric receptors. The study mapped resistance genetically to the α6 locus and confirmed causality by expressing wild-type versus mis-spliced variants in *Xenopus* oocytes. Critically, the wild-type binding site geometry is intact in susceptible insects, and the agonist-binding interface is located in the accessible extracellular N-terminal domain (ECD) — properties that are directly relevant to peptide binder design.

## Key claims

- Pxα6 is the confirmed sole target of spinosad in *P. xylostella*.
- The field-evolved resistance mechanism is exon skipping, which is orthogonal to the agonist-binding site — the wild-type binding site geometry is intact and functionally usable.
- The agonist-binding interface resides in the extracellular N-terminal domain (ECD), making it well-accessible for peptide binders.
- Spinosad resistance is geographically widespread (multiple continents), confirming Pxα6 as a high-priority agronomic target.
- Causality was established by *Xenopus* oocyte expression of WT vs. mis-spliced variants.

## Evidence and examples

- Genetic mapping placed resistance at the Pxα6 locus across multiple independent field populations.
- *Xenopus* oocyte functional assays confirmed that WT Pxα6 confers spinosad sensitivity; mis-spliced variants do not.
- Exon skipping was observed consistently across geographically distinct resistant populations, suggesting convergent evolution toward the same mechanism.
